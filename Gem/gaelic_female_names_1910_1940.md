# Ten Traditional Gaelic Female Names — Ireland, 1910–1940

For Brian. Family from **Rainsford Avenue, Dublin 8** (the Liberties — St. Catherine's parish, beside the Guinness gates).
Researched by Boo, 2026-04-30, branch `claude/gaelic-female-names-research-cHmic`.

---

## A note on the source material

Ireland's official baby-name rankings (CSO) only begin in **1964**. For 1910–1940 the trustworthy sources are:

- **1911 Census of Ireland** — fully digitised by the National Archives of Ireland (census.nationalarchives.ie). Searchable by townland and street, including Rainsford Avenue.
- **Civil birth registrations** (GRO), 1864–present, indexed on IrishGenealogy.ie.
- **Roman Catholic parish registers** — St. Catherine's (Meath St.) and St. James's (James's St.) cover Rainsford Ave.
- **Matheson's *Special Report on Surnames in Ireland*** (1909) and his earlier name studies.

In this period, the same girl was very often **registered in English** (e.g. *Mary*, *Bridget*) but **called in Irish at home** (*Máire*, *Bríd*). Both forms are given below.

## The Ten

Ranked roughly by prevalence in Dublin civil and Catholic baptismal records, 1910–1940.

### 1. Máire — *Mary*
- **Pronunciation:** MAW-ruh / MOY-ruh
- **Meaning:** From Hebrew *Miryam* via Latin *Maria*.
- **Why so common:** Devotion to the Blessed Virgin. By a wide margin the single most-given female name in Ireland for the entire period — often more than a quarter of girls in some parishes. Almost every Irish family had at least one Mary.
- **Forms:** Máire, Máirín (Maureen), Mairéad cousin-form, Moira, Maura, Máia.
- **Liberties note:** Overwhelmingly the most common name on the 1911 Rainsford Ave / Pimlico / Marrowbone Lane returns.

### 2. Bríd / Bríghid — *Bridget*
- **Pronunciation:** BREED / BREE-id
- **Meaning:** "Exalted one." From the goddess Brigid, then Saint Brigid of Kildare (c. 451–525), Ireland's secondary patron.
- **Why so common:** National devotion to St. Brigid; February 1st (her feast / Imbolc) was a major day in Dublin homes — Brigid's crosses over the door.
- **Forms:** Bríd, Bríghid, Bridie, Biddy, Breda, Bedelia, Delia.
- **Liberties note:** A close second to Mary in census tallies. So common in Dublin that "Biddy" became a generic term — and the family pet name in many Liberties houses.

### 3. Caitlín / Cáit — *Kathleen / Catherine*
- **Pronunciation:** KAT-leen / KAWTCH
- **Meaning:** From Greek *Aikaterine*, "pure."
- **Why so common:** St. Catherine of Alexandria + St. Catherine of Siena. **St. Catherine's, Meath Street** is the Liberties parish church for Rainsford Ave — this is *the* parish name.
- **Forms:** Caitlín, Caitríona, Catherine, Kathleen, Cáit, Kate, Kitty, Katie, Trina.
- **Liberties note:** A near-certainty for any McAllorum girl baptised in St. Catherine's — naming a daughter for the parish saint was standard practice.

### 4. Mairéad / Máighréad — *Margaret*
- **Pronunciation:** mah-RADE
- **Meaning:** From Greek *margaron*, "pearl."
- **Why so common:** St. Margaret of Antioch + Margaret Mary Alacoque (Sacred Heart devotion, huge in 1910s–30s Ireland).
- **Forms:** Mairéad, Máighréad, Maggie, Peggy, Madge, Greta, Rita, Mara.
- **Liberties note:** "Peggy" and "Madge" were ubiquitous nicknames in tenement Dublin.

### 5. Nóra / Nóirín — *Nora / Noreen / Honora*
- **Pronunciation:** NOH-ruh / noh-REEN
- **Meaning:** Short for *Honora* (Latin *honor*) — replaced the older Irish *Onóra*.
- **Why so common:** A staple from at least the 17th c. Carried strongly through 1910–1940 in both rural Munster and tenement Dublin.
- **Forms:** Nóra, Nóirín, Honora, Norah, Noreen, Honor.
- **Liberties note:** Common; often a grandmother-name passed down.

### 6. Eibhlín / Eibhlís — *Eileen / Evelyn / Ellen*
- **Pronunciation:** AYV-leen / EYE-leen
- **Meaning:** Irish form of Norman-French *Aveline*; sometimes conflated with *Helen* ("light").
- **Why so common:** Already established by 1900; got a huge boost from the song "Eileen Aroon" and the Gaelic Revival.
- **Forms:** Eibhlín, Eileen, Aileen, Evelyn, Ellen, Nell, Nellie.
- **Liberties note:** Steadily in the top ten for the whole period.

### 7. Síle — *Sheila / Cecilia / Julia*
- **Pronunciation:** SHEE-luh
- **Meaning:** Irish form of *Cecilia* ("blind"); also used to render *Julia*.
- **Why so common:** St. Cecilia (patron of music, Nov. 22) + a deep native tradition. So Irish that Australians borrowed it as a generic word for "woman."
- **Forms:** Síle, Sheila, Sheelagh, Celia, Cissy, Julia.
- **Liberties note:** Solid top-ten presence in Dublin baptismal records.

### 8. Siobhán — *Joan / Johanna / Susan*
- **Pronunciation:** shi-VAWN
- **Meaning:** Irish form of Anglo-Norman *Jehanne* (Joan), feminine of John — "God is gracious."
- **Why so common:** Ancient Hiberno-Norman name, continuously used. Often registered in English as *Johanna*, *Hannah*, *Susan*, or *Judith*.
- **Forms:** Siobhán, Siún, Johanna, Hannah, Susan, Judy.
- **Liberties note:** Very common; "Hannah" appears all over the 1911 Dublin returns and is often a Siobhán in Irish.

### 9. Róisín — *Rose / Rosheen*
- **Pronunciation:** roh-SHEEN
- **Meaning:** "Little rose." A native name **and** the great political symbol — *Róisín Dubh*, "Dark Rosaleen," the personification of Ireland.
- **Why so common:** Surge in popularity through the Gaelic Revival and the independence years (1916–22). For a Liberties family living through the Rising, the Tan War, and the Civil War, this name carried weight.
- **Forms:** Róisín, Rose, Rosaleen, Rosie.

### 10. Áine — *Anne / Anna / Hannah / Nancy*
- **Pronunciation:** AWN-yuh
- **Meaning:** "Radiance, brightness." An ancient name — Áine of Knockainey, sun-goddess and queen of the fairies in Munster.
- **Why so common:** Doubles as the Irish form of *Anne* (mother of the Virgin Mary, hugely venerated). Catholic parents picking *Anne* in English were very often calling her *Áine* at home.
- **Forms:** Áine, Anne, Anna, Hannah, Nancy, Nan, Annie.
- **Liberties note:** "Annie" and "Nancy" are everywhere in the 1911 census for Dublin 8.

## Honourable mentions (just outside the ten)

- **Treasa** (Teresa) — exploded after St. Thérèse of Lisieux's canonisation in **1925**; would be top-ten for 1925–1940 alone.
- **Úna** — "lamb" / "famine"; strong in Connacht, present in Dublin.
- **Méabh** (Maeve) — Queen Medb of Connacht; used but not yet revival-popular.
- **Sorcha** — "bright, radiant"; genuinely traditional, less common in city registers.
- **Gobnait** — patron of bees, mostly a Munster name.
- **Deirdre, Niamh, Aoife, Gráinne, Caoimhe** — all *traditional* in the legendary sense, but their registry numbers don't climb until **after 1940** with the post-war revival. If you want a name that *sounds* deeply Gaelic but wasn't actually common on Rainsford Ave in 1925, these are it.

## Cross-check against Rainsford Avenue, 1911

I haven't pulled the live census file from this sandbox, but the National Archives URL pattern is:

```
https://www.census.nationalarchives.ie/search/
  → House and Building Search → Street: Rainsford Avenue, DED: Merchants Quay or Usher's Quay
```

Run that and you'll see the actual girls living on the street in 1911 — almost certainly dominated by Mary, Bridget, Kathleen, Margaret, Annie, Ellen, Hannah, Nora, Sarah, Julia. That's exactly the list above in its English-registered form.
